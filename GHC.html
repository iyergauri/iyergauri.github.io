GHC Essay Questions

1. What do you think is one problem in the world that could be solved by technology? How do you propose solving it?

I’ll be using the term “womxn” in this answer to be intentionally inclusive of trans women.

The lack of diversity in tech is frustrating, and it will not be solved with better hiring practices and reducing implicit bias in the workplace. In my wildest dreams, 50.8% of engineers would be womxn, reflective of the world population. But only 18% of college graduates in computer science are womxn, so the bottleneck starts before unfair hiring practices: why aren’t more womxn studying computer science? Research indicates that there are a whole host of reasons, including a “chilly” university classroom climate, a “dearth” of role models, not enough established womxn to have resources for female and non-binary employees, and a lack of preparation. This seems paradoxical, since essentially the reason it’s difficult to encourage womxn to go into computing is because there aren’t many womxn in computing right now.

My solution is to introduce nationwide, online, free workbooks for K-12 teachers to foster systemic change. Creating a web-based curriculum for K-12 teachers will provide in-classroom exposure, leveling the playing field across gender, since students will no longer be responsible for finding their own resources. MOOCs and bootcamps are awesome but it’s still up to the learner to find them. School districts across the country are desperate to have computer science taught in their classrooms, but many teachers aren’t qualified to teach it, and computer science graduates rarely teach at the K-12 level.

Early exposure to programming via an interactive, comprehensive, web-based workbook that is intuitive for teachers AND engaging for learners is key. A mandatory, standardized introduction to creative and open-ended programming will inspire more girls to consider computing as a career.

2. The travel scholarship program is open to all qualified industry professionals and students and is committed to promoting diversity within the technology industry. This year's scholarship has a dedicated focus on engaging women of color. This includes but is not limited to women of Black/AfricanAmerican/Caribbean/WestIndies and Hispanic/Latinx descent. How has your experience as a student, intern or industry professional informed your understanding of women in tech, either personally or peripherally? What campus and/or community organizations are you involved in that demonstrate your passion for increasing representation in tech?

I’ll be using the term “womxn” in this answer to be intentionally inclusive of trans women.

Engineers and developers are still not testing and debugging with womxn in user tests, as if they don’t exist at all. There are countless examples of how technology is made for men, by men: smartphones are so big they hurt womxn’s hands, squeeze/pressure features overestimate womxn’s strength, models are accidentally trained to filter out womxn’s resume...

Joining tech companies to be the change we wish to see is also so hard. At a previous internship I was the only person of color AND the only woman. All summer long, I was deeply aware of my “otherness.” It was so easy for workplace microaggressions, casual misogyny, and blatant sexualization to exist. When I wanted to stand up for myself, I felt like I was hugely outnumbered; I also didn’t want to be seen as dramatic or cowardly.

My experience as a woman in tech, combined with my passion for representation, has culminated in loads of on-campus work. I’ve tutored data structures classes, TA’d a class for future computer science educators about computational thinking, worked for the Center for Digital Learning creating introductory computer science materials, and served as the Volunteer Coordinator for an outreach program called CSForEach. CSForEach at UCSD would bring free workshops, speaker series, and in-class support to libraries and K-12 schools in the lower San Diego county areas, close to the border, where there was less funding.
